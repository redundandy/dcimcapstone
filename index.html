Some Common Consumer Cybersecurity Tools and How They Actually Work

VPNs

You've probably heard about VPNs, they are widely used nowadays. In 2025, CNET found that 47% of adults in the US use a VPN.

Image retrieved from CNET

When you connect to a VPN, your device and the VPN server quickly perform a "handshake" to exchange encryption keys, ensuring only they can decrypt each other‚Äôs data. This creates a secure tunnel through which your encrypted data travels to the VPN server, where it is decrypted and sent to the website you're visiting. The website's response is then encrypted again by the VPN server and sent back through the tunnel to your device, where it's finally decrypted. As a result, anyone trying to monitor your activity will only see encrypted, unreadable information (Tomaschek & Long, 2024).

It is important to note that ‚Äúwhile using a VPN means all that information is hidden from your ISP, the VPN provider can see it all instead ‚Ä¶[and it is] hard to judge how well any of the hundreds of VPNs on the market take care of your data,‚Äù (Grauer, 2021).

A Brief Case Study

A field study was conducted of over a hundred thousand problem descriptions that was sent to the customer support department of an organization that provides antivirus software. They compared user diagnoses of the problem to what experts believed the problems were. In the end, the article concluded, "we found that users and experts agreed for most types of issues, except for attacks (e.g., malware infections). The disagreements, however, uncovered several misconceptions that may expose users to risks (e.g., falling victim to technical support and fake AV scams),‚Äù (Sharif et al., 2019)

This paper highlights a disconnect between how individuals perceive cybersecurity and how security technologies actually function. It also points out that adding a security tool can sometimes lead users to engage in riskier behavior than they normally would, ultimately making the technology more harmful than helpful.

Influencer marketing has turned VPNs into trendy must-haves, often pitched as simple fixes for online privacy. What Can We Do?

To stay truly protected, consumers need to go beyond flashy ads and develop a basic understanding of how cybersecurity tools actually work. This means doing a bit of research before trusting bold claims and recognizing that no tool is foolproof. I would recommend users to look for transparency in a provider‚Äôs privacy policy, read independent reviews from trusted sources, and understand their own security habits in relation to the tools.
